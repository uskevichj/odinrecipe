Steak

Steak is a meat generally sliced perpendicular to the muscle fibers, potentially including a bone. Exceptions, in which the meat is sliced parallel to the fibers, include the skirt steak cut from the plate, the flank steak cut from the abdominal muscles, and the Silverfinger steak cut from the loin and includes three rib bones. When the word "steak" is used without qualification, it generally refers to a beefsteak. In a larger sense, there are also fish steaks, ground meat steaks, pork steak and many more varieties of steaks.

Steak is usually grilled, but can be pan-fried. It is often grilled in an attempt to replicate the flavor of steak cooked over the glowing coals of an open fire. Steak can also be cooked in sauce, such as in steak and kidney pie, or minced and formed into patties, such as hamburgers.

Steaks are often cut from grazing animals, usually farmed, other than cattle, including bison, camel, goat, horse, kangaroo, sheep, ostrich, pigs, reindeer, turkey and deer, as well as various types of fish, especially salmon and large pelagic fish such as swordfish, shark and marlin. Some cured meat, such as gammon, is commonly served as steak.

Recipe

  • Ingredients

    • 1 (2-pound) porterhouse steak, 1 1/2 inches thick
    • Kosher salt
    • Ground cracked black peppercorn

  • Directions

    1. Preheat the oven to 450 degrees F.
    2. Heat a large cast iron skillet over high heat for 5 to 7 minutes.
    3. Pat the steak dry with paper towels. Season the steak on both sides with salt and pepper.
    4. When the skillet is screaming hot, add the steak. Cook until a golden brown crust forms, about 4 to 5 minutes.
    5. Flip the steak and put the skillet in the oven. Cook until the steak is medium rare, about 6 to 8 minutes.
    6. Remove the steak from the skillet and let it rest for 10 minutes before slicing.
